Movie Licensing
for Public Events

Planning an outdoor movie event? Here's everything you need to know about public performance licenses — what they are, who needs one, what they cost, and where to get one.

Jump to: What Is It? Do I Need One? Who's Responsible? Penalties Cost How to Get One FAQ

The Basics

What Is a Public Performance Movie License?

A public performance license (sometimes called a movie license or PPL) is the legal right to show a copyrighted film to a public audience. When you purchase a movie on DVD, Blu-ray, or through a streaming service, you are purchasing the right to watch it privately — not to project it in front of a crowd.

Movie licensing exists to ensure that filmmakers, studios, and rights holders are fairly compensated when their work is shown to groups of people. Think of it as a "group rate" for the content — if many people are going to watch it together at an event, a licensing fee applies.

The specific license you need for most outdoor events is a public performance license, which grants you the right to display a specific film to a public audience on a specific date.

Do You Need One?

Who Needs a Movie License?

The short answer: if you are showing a copyrighted movie to a public audience, you need a license. This applies regardless of whether you charge admission, how many people attend, or whether your organization is a nonprofit.

Common events that require a public performance license include:

HOA movie nights, park and recreation events, school and university screenings, corporate events and team-building gatherings, church and community events, drive-in movie events, summer camps, libraries, hospitals, and any event where the general public or a group larger than a private gathering is invited to watch a film.

What the Law Says

The Motion Picture Association of America states: "The Federal Copyright Act (Title 17 of the US Code) governs how copyrighted materials, such as movies, may be used. Neither the rental nor the purchase of a copy of a copyrighted work carries with it the right to publicly exhibit the work."

"Bars, restaurants, private clubs, prisons, lodges, factories, summer camps, public libraries, daycare facilities, parks and recreation departments, churches, and non-classroom use at schools and universities are all examples of situations where a public performance license must be obtained."

Private events — such as watching a movie with a small group of friends or family on private property — generally do not require a license. But as soon as the event becomes public or organizational in nature, the licensing requirement kicks in.

Responsibility

Who Is Responsible for Getting the License?

The organization or individual hosting the event is responsible for obtaining the public performance license — not the company providing the screen or equipment.

If you rent an outdoor movie screen from Premiere Outdoor Movies, we bring the screen, projector, sound system, and crew. But the film license is your responsibility. We can help point you in the right direction, but the license must be secured by the event organizer before the show begins.

This is a common point of confusion, so it's worth repeating: the screen rental company is not responsible for movie licensing. The entity hosting the event carries that obligation.

Legal Consequences

What Happens If You Don't Have a License?

⚠️ Know the Risks

According to the Motion Picture Association of America: "Willful infringement of these rules is a federal crime carrying a maximum sentence of up to five years in jail and/or a $250,000 fine. Even inadvertent infringement is subject to substantial civil damages."

Will you end up in federal prison for screening a movie at your HOA event without a license? Probably not. But civil damages are a real possibility, and the cost of a license is a fraction of what you could face in legal fees or settlements.

More importantly, obtaining a license is simply the right thing to do. The people who made the film deserve to be compensated when their work is shown publicly. Licensing is affordable, easy, and gives you complete peace of mind so you can focus on what matters — putting on a great event.

Pricing

How Much Does a Movie License Cost?

Movie licensing fees vary based on several factors: the specific film, the number of attendees, whether tickets are sold, the type of venue, and the licensing provider. Here's a general range to help you budget:

$300–$500
Small Events
Under 200 attendees. HOA nights, school events, backyard community screenings.
$500–$1,000
Mid-Size Events
200–500 attendees. Parks, corporate events, community gatherings.
$1,000+
Large Events
500+ attendees or ticketed events. Festivals, drive-ins, large public screenings.

Newer releases and blockbuster titles tend to cost more to license than older or independent films. The licensing providers can give you an exact quote once you specify the title and event details.

How to Get Licensed

Where to Purchase a Public Performance License

There are two primary providers of public performance movie licenses in the United States. Both are reputable, easy to work with, and can typically turn around a license quickly once you provide the event details.

Swank Motion Pictures
One of the largest providers of public performance licenses in the country. Swank works with schools, parks, corporations, and community organizations. Visit swank.com to get a quote and purchase your license online.
Criterion Pictures
Another leading provider of movie licensing for public screenings. Criterion handles a wide range of film titles and event types. Visit criterionpicusa.com to request licensing for your event.

When contacting either provider, have the following ready: the film title, your event date, estimated attendance, venue type, and whether admission will be charged. They'll handle the rest and provide you with the documentation you need.

Common Questions

Movie Licensing FAQ

Do I need a license if I'm not charging admission?
Yes. The requirement for a public performance license applies regardless of whether you charge admission. Free public events still require a license if a copyrighted film is being shown.
Does Premiere Outdoor Movies provide the movie license?
No. We provide the screen, projector, audio system, and professional crew — but the movie license is the responsibility of the event organizer. We're happy to point you toward the right licensing providers.
Can I use Netflix, Disney+, or another streaming service for my outdoor event?
No. Streaming service licenses are for personal, private use only. Displaying streaming content at a public event violates the terms of service of every major streaming platform and still requires a separate public performance license.
How far in advance should I get my license?
We recommend securing your license at least 2 weeks before your event, though many providers can turn around licenses more quickly if needed. Don't leave it to the last minute — some popular titles have limited availability.
What if the film I want isn't available for licensing?
Some newly released films may not yet be available for public performance licensing. If your first choice isn't available, the licensing provider can suggest alternatives. Films that are older or in the public domain may have different licensing situations.
Does my license cover multiple showings?
Typically, a public performance license covers a single screening on a specific date. If you want to show the same film multiple times or on multiple dates, you'll need a separate license for each screening or a multi-screening agreement.
